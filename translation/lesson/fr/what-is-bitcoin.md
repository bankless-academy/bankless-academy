# A Brief History of Money

Money has been an integral part of society since our earliest days, where beads, shells and stones were used to exchange goods & services.

Yet early currencies had a problem: it was too easy for someone to find/create new money. The more of something there is, the less value it has, and so everyone‚Äôs money slowly became worth less. If currency was to hold its value, it needed to be harder to find.

Materials like gold & silver were eventually adopted to solve this problem. Their `scarcity` meant that it would be difficult for anyone to add more money to the existing supply.

As societies grew, a government‚Äôs central bank would issue standardized coins made of gold & silver. Such currency made money recognizable, measurable, while serving as a strong store of value.

![](https://app.banklessacademy.com/images/what-is-bitcoin/a-brief-history-of-money-50cded5e.svg)

# A Brief History of Money (Pt. 2)

With the arrival of the 20th century came the fall of the ‚Äú`Gold Standard`‚Äù. Precious metals were slowly disconnected from currencies across the world ‚Äî an effort that would allow any country‚Äôs economy to grow beyond its supply of gold. As a central bank no longer needs gold to create currency, they can create as much new money as they deem necessary for their country‚Äôs economy.

Electronic currency has also largely replaced physical currency ‚Äî with most money now stored in commercial banks. While convenient and secure, it has also put a for-profit business between people and their money.

![](https://app.banklessacademy.com/images/what-is-bitcoin/a-brief-history-of-money-pt-2-1c7f4b4f.svg)

# The Problem

The money we are left with today is no longer truly owned by the people. It is largely under the control of the bank, with its value determined by the central bank. This opens a whole host of problems we will explore throughout this lesson.

With this type of money, you cannot make free financial choices in your life, nor enjoy reliable financial security. You are always subject to being denied access to your money, while the central bank‚Äôs printing policy slowly erodes your savings.

***

For money to once again be neutral and reliable, it must regain the two key properties it has lost:

üíé **Value:** It must reliably store value over time, so that you aren‚Äôt forced to spend it.

‚úä **Ownership:** It must give you sole, unrestricted control over your funds, so they can‚Äôt be confiscated without due legal process.

Only these qualities can return financial freedom & security to modern money.

# Enter Bitcoin

`Satoshi Nakamoto` believed that exchange of value shouldn‚Äôt be controlled by small groups. He sought to build money with `neutrality` and reliability built into the system. And he did.

Enter Bitcoin, the digital currency powering global transactions completely outside of the traditional banking system.

- ‚õìÔ∏è Using `blockchain` technology, it connects people directly, rather than going through institutions. It‚Äôs fully automated, neutral, and highly resistant to fraud and manipulation.
- üíé With no central authority to print money, bitcoin has `scarcity` and value-retention similar to gold.
- ‚úä With no institution facilitating transfer or storage, the currency returns control to the holder. It‚Äôs `self-custodial`: holders store their own funds in a secure online safe.

For these reasons, bitcoin has earned the nickname ‚Äúdigital gold‚Äù.

![](https://app.banklessacademy.com/images/what-is-bitcoin/enter-bitcoin-efba6ccd.svg)

# Digital Gold: Scarcity

Gold is naturally difficult to extract from the earth. In a similar way, undiscovered bitcoin can only be introduced through a difficult process called digital `mining`. There is also a set maximum of 21 million bitcoin ever available to be discovered, mirroring gold‚Äôs finite availability in the earth.

This `scarcity` makes both very effective at retaining value, as new supply is difficult to introduce.

***

In traditional currencies, a central bank will introduce between 3% - 15% new currency every year. This supply increase leads to `inflation`, the devaluation of existing money. Inflation is why everything seems to be getting more expensive lately. Goods and services aren‚Äôt necessarily worth more, money is just worth less because there‚Äôs more of it. As prices rise, most salaries remain the same, creating a higher cost of living for the average person.

To add insult to injury, new supply is typically distributed to the banks, further increasing the wealth divide between the lower, middle and upper classes.

# How does it work?

Let‚Äôs switch gears and look at the engine powering Bitcoin.

Bitcoin runs on a shared network, hosted across thousands of computers worldwide. Each computer on the network does its small part in keeping the network running; The network is the computers! If one produces an error, the others are there to take its place.

This also means there‚Äôs no single point to apply pressure in attempted hacks; In order for the network to be attacked, a majority of the computers would need to be compromised by one group of people.

This defense strategy is called `decentralization`: the shifting of control from one main group to many smaller ones. Decentralization is what makes cryptocurrency transfers virtually unstoppable.

![](https://app.banklessacademy.com/images/what-is-bitcoin/how-does-it-work-cf820a7a.svg)

# How does it work? (Pt. 2)

When sending cryptocurrency across this network, your transaction is checked and validated by these `decentralized` network participants. These participants, known as `miners`, are constantly organizing transactions and adding them to a database hosted across the network.

This process is known as `mining`. It‚Äôs what keeps the network running securely. Mining can get quite complicated, so we‚Äôll take a simplified view:

1. You submit a transaction to the network, sending 0.1 `BTC` to your friend.
2. A miner is randomly selected to check your transaction for errors.
3. Other miners double-check the results to ensure accuracy.
4. If approved, your transaction is added to the database.
5. Your friend receives 0.1 BTC in their `cryptocurrency wallet`.
